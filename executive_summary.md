## What We Found

### 1. Spending Drives Outcomes — But Diminishing Returns Exist
Countries that spend more on healthcare live longer. Statistical analysis found a **strong positive correlation (r = 0.66)** between per-capita health spending and life expectancy. However, the USA spends 3x more than Europe yet has similar — often worse — outcomes, revealing **systemic inefficiency** in high-income countries.

### 2. Child Mortality Is the Most Urgent Crisis
Under-5 mortality in Sub-Saharan Africa averages **94.6 per 1,000 live births** — nearly **18x higher** than in North America (5.1). This gap is almost entirely driven by preventable infections, malnutrition, and lack of vaccine access.

### 3. The Healthcare Access Gap Is the Root Driver
Access Index scores reveal a stark divide:

- **North America & Europe:** ~88/100
- **Sub-Saharan Africa:** 31.6/100

Countries with access scores below 50 face a cycle of crisis: sickness leads to poverty, which leads to less investment in health, which leads to more sickness.

### 4. Vaccination Is One of the Highest-ROI Interventions
Vaccination coverage correlates with reduced child mortality (r = -0.58). Expanding vaccination programs to 95%+ coverage in low-income nations could prevent an estimated **1.5 million child deaths per year**.

## Investment Case

> **For every $1 invested in healthcare in low-income countries, there is an estimated $4 return** through increased productivity, reduced catastrophic health expenditure, and economic stability (WHO, 2021).

Closing the healthcare funding gap in Sub-Saharan Africa alone (~$378/person shortfall for 1.2 billion people) would require approximately **$450 billion** over 10 years — less than 0.5% of global GDP annually.
